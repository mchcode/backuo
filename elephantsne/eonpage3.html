Mammoths and Mastodons

At different times during the Cenozoic, many types of elephants roamed the Nebraska landscape. Four-tuskers, Stegomastodons, Mastodons, and Mammoths were some of the most common elephants in Nebraska during the CenozoicThe earliest elephant found in Nebraska is the four-tusked elephant that migrated from Asia to North America. The mammoths (elephants) did not reach our Nebraska, on the southern edge of the glaciers, until the late Pleistocene Epoch, about one half million years ago. Skeletal remains of elephants have been found in all parts of Nebraska, and many have been preserved in the world famous Elephant Hall, on the campus of the University of Nebraska in Lincoln. "It is estimated that there are at least 10 prehistoric elephant remains, on average, per square mile buried in Nebraska---more than there are living elephants in Africa." http://www.mccookgazette.com/story/1097462.html
Savanna and grassland vegetation dominated the landscapeof Nebraska during the time of the four-tuskers and stegamastodon. Four-tuskers: Four-tuskers migrated to North America from Asia and were present in Nebraska during the Middle and Late Miocene. They are the oldest elephant known to exist in Nebraska, Many of the four-tuskers remains have been found in north-central Nebraska near the town of Valentine. Stegomastodon: Stegomastodon existed in Nebraska during the Pliocene They became extinct about 4 million years . Stegomastodon remains have been found at many localities throughout Nebraska.
The climate in Nebraska was much cooler and drier because of the ice age in the Northern Hemisphere, which started around 1.8 million years ago. Mastodons: Mastodons were present in Nebraska from 2 million until about 10,000 years ago. Mastodons ate shrubs and pine trees in a grassland-dominated ecosystem. Remains of Mastodons are found in the glacial deposits in the eastern part of Nebraska. More bull dog in build than elephant. Mammoths: Mammoths are the most commonly found elephant fossil in Nebraska. Mammoths were present in Nebraska from ~ 2 million until about 10,000 years ago. Various species of mammoth crossed the Bering Strait land bridge about and spread throughout most of North America.
The world's largest Mammoth skeleton was found in Lincoln County, Nebraska; nicknamed "Archie." "When he was alive Archie must have been something to behold. His height, at the shoulder, was 14', and he was still growing when he died. compares with eight or nine feet for modern circus elephants. His tusks were almost 13 feet long, and measured 33 in circumference at the base. He would have weighed in at about 15 tons. http://www.mccookgazette.com/story/1097462.html He is on display in the University of Nebraska State Museum's world famous Elephant Hall.

Mammoths

Although the mammoth was an elephant, it was much larger than the modern elephant. Three main species that have been found in Nebraska. are the Elephas primigenius, the Elephas columbi or Columbian elephant, and the Elephas imperator or Imperial elephant. The Elephas primigenius, what we recognize as a Wooly Mammoth, had a coat of long, shaggy hair to protect it from the extreme cold of the north, and its remains are seldom found south of Nebraska.
Woolly mammoths evolved a suite of adaptations for arctic life, including morphological traits such as small ears and tails to minimize heat loss, a thick layer of subcutaneous fat, long thick fur, and numerous sebaceous glands for insulation , as well as a large brown-fat hump like deposit behind the neck that may have functioned as a heat source and fat reservoir during winter.[21]Vincent Lynch 2 july 2015 "Elephantid Genomes Reveal the Molecular Bases of Wooly Mammoth Adaptions to the Artic" Cell Reports (Edit to fill left text box)

Where Did They Go

There is still an active debate on the extinction of elephants in North America. There are three sides to the discussion. Some say they were hunted to extinction. Another side claims their extinction was the result of climate change which killed the once abundant food source. The third camp suggests that it was a combination of the two. The confusion comes from the arrival of humans at the same time that climate change was changing the landscape. Grasslands were changed to forests and other areas became hot and arid, destroying the grazing grounds of the great herbivores.
